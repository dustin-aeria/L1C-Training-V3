## Question 1
**Category:** L1C08 Radiotelephony | **Bloom's Level:** Application

What is the Air Traffic Frequency (ATF) in use for the Tillsonburg (CYTB) aerodrome?

- ☐ a) 123.2
- ☐ b) 123.0
- ☐ c) 126.7
- ☐ d) 121.5

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: b) 123.0 MHz.** This must be looked up in the Canada Flight Supplement (CFS). Students should recognize that each aerodrome has its own published MF or ATF — you cannot assume a standard frequency. 123.2 is the default for uncontrolled aerodromes without a published frequency, 126.7 is the VFR en route frequency, and 121.5 is the emergency frequency. None of these are the specific ATF for Tillsonburg.

**Why this matters:** During site survey for L1C operations, you must identify the frequencies in use at all aerodromes within and near your operating area. If you are monitoring the wrong frequency, you miss traffic information that is critical for DAA.

**Reference:** Canada Flight Supplement (NAV CANADA); L1C08 Radiotelephony — Key Frequencies

</details>

## Question 2
**Category:** L1C07 Theory of Flight | **Bloom's Level:** Application

A fixed-wing Remotely Piloted Aircraft (RPA) that has been loaded with an aft centre of gravity has a higher-than-normal stall speed.

- ☐ a) True
- ☐ b) False

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: b) False.** An aft CG actually produces a LOWER stall speed (not higher) because the tail produces less downward force, reducing the total lift required. However, the aft CG is FAR more dangerous despite the lower stall speed because recovery from a stall may be impossible — the nose-up tendency can exceed the elevator's authority to push the nose down. A FORWARD CG produces a higher stall speed.

**Common misconception:** Students often confuse forward and aft CG effects. Remember: Forward CG = higher stall speed but more stable. Aft CG = lower stall speed but potentially unrecoverable stall — much more dangerous.

**Reference:** *From the Ground Up*, "Airmanship" (MacDonald, 2025); L1C07 Theory of Flight — Centre of Gravity

</details>

## Question 3
**Category:** L1C05 Navigation | **Bloom's Level:** Recall

Select all the CORRECT chart-to-scale matches.

- ☐ a) VNC (VFR Navigation Chart) → 1:500,000
- ☐ b) WAC (World Aeronautical Chart) → 1:500,000
- ☐ c) VTA (VFR Terminal Area) → 1:250,000
- ☐ d) WAC (World Aeronautical Chart) → 1:1,000,000

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: a), c), and d)** The correct matches are: VNC → 1:500,000, VTA → 1:250,000, WAC → 1:1,000,000. Option b) is incorrect — WAC is 1:1,000,000, not 1:500,000.

**Memory aid:** VTA is the most detailed (smallest scale number = largest detail). VNC is the standard for L1C planning. WAC is the broadest overview. Think: VTA zooms IN, WAC zooms OUT, VNC is in the middle.

</details>

## Question 4
**Category:** L1C05 Navigation | **Bloom's Level:** Recall

What is a critical point?

- ☐ a) A point along the route from which it is an equal time to turn back or to continue to the destination
- ☐ b) The point during a flight when it is no longer possible to Return to Home (RTH)
- ☐ c) The point with the centre of gravity that is in balance

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: a)** The critical point (also called the equal time point) is the point along the route from which it takes the same amount of time to continue to the destination as it does to return to the departure point. Before the critical point, returning is faster; after it, continuing is faster. This calculation accounts for wind.

**Do not confuse with:** The Point of No Return (PNR), which is the point beyond which the aircraft does not have sufficient energy to return. The critical point is about TIME; the PNR is about ENERGY.

**Reference:** *From the Ground Up*, "Air Navigation" (MacDonald, 2025); L1C05 Navigation — Critical Point

</details>

## Question 5
**Category:** L1C05 Navigation | **Bloom's Level:** Application

What is the height of the tower at 53° 32' N 113° 47' W?

- ☐ a) 895ft Above Ground Level (AGL)
- ☐ b) 895m AGL
- ☐ c) 3,255m Above Sea Level (ASL)
- ☐ d) 349ft AGL
- ☐ e) 2,620ft ASL
- ☐ f) None of the above

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: a) 895ft AGL.** This requires looking up the specific tower on an Edmonton VNC or VTA chart at the given coordinates. On aeronautical charts, obstacle heights are typically shown in two numbers — the top number is the height ASL and the bottom number (in parentheses or smaller) is the height AGL. Students must be able to read both values and distinguish between them.

**Why this matters:** When planning a BVLOS route, every obstacle along the path must be identified with its AGL height. If a tower is 895 ft AGL and your maximum flight altitude is 400 ft AGL, you must route around it — and your operational volume must not intersect with it.

**Reference:** Edmonton VNC or Edmonton VTA; L1C05 Navigation — VNC Components

</details>

## Question 6
**Category:** L1C05 Navigation | **Bloom's Level:** Recall

What is the Point of No Return (PNR) in aeronautical navigation?

- ☐ a) The point during a flight when it is no longer possible to Return to Home (RTH)
- ☐ b) The point along the route from which it is an equal time to turn back or to continue to the destination
- ☐ c) A bad movie from 1993 starring Bridget Fonda

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: a)** The PNR is the point during a flight when it is no longer possible to return home with the remaining energy. Beyond this point, the aircraft must continue to the destination or an alternate landing site. The PNR formula is: Time to PNR = (E × O) / (O + H), where E = endurance, O = groundspeed out, H = groundspeed home.

**Do not confuse with:** The Critical Point (option b), which is about equal TIME, not energy. The PNR can be calculated and is a hard limit — exceeding it means the aircraft cannot return.

**Reference:** *From the Ground Up*, "Air Navigation" (MacDonald, 2025); L1C05 Navigation — Point of No Return

</details>

## Question 7
**Category:** L1C05 Navigation | **Bloom's Level:** Application

Your proposed operational volume extends from between 20 Nautical Miles (NM) to 24 NM west of Drayton Valley, Alberta (Brazeau County). What is the population density according to the 2021 census data?

- ☐ a) 2.4 people per km²
- ☐ b) 236 people per km²
- ☐ c) 5 people per km²
- ☐ d) 25 people per km²

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: a) 2.4 people per km².** This requires looking up the actual census data for Brazeau County on Statistics Canada. At 2.4 pp/km², this area is classified as "unpopulated" (below the 5 pp/km² threshold), meaning 901.87(a) applies and a standard SAD is sufficient for L1C operations.

**Why this matters:** Population density determines which 901.87 pathway your operation falls under, which in turn determines what RPAS declaration is required. Getting this wrong means operating under the wrong regulatory pathway — a compliance violation.

**Reference:** Statistics Canada, Brazeau County, Municipal district [Census subdivision], Alberta; L1C05 Navigation — Population Density Determination

</details>

## Question 8

In Part IX of the *Canadian Aviation Regulations (CARs)*, a populated area is any area with more than how many people per square kilometre?

- ☐ b) 10 people per km²
- ☐ c) 25 people per km²
- ☐ d) 50 people per km²

<details>

**Answer: c)** More than 25 people per km² = populated area. Below 5 pp/km² = unpopulated. Between 5 and 25 pp/km² = sparsely populated. Above 25 pp/km² = populated.

## Question 9
**Category:** L1C05 Navigation | **Bloom's Level:** Recall

Part IX of *Canadian Aviation Regulations (CARs)* defines a sparsely populated area as an area of between 5 and how many people per square kilometre?

- ☐ a) 10 people per km²

- ☐ c) 25 people per km²
- ☐ d) 50 people per km²

## Question 10
**Category:** L1C08 Radiotelephony | **Bloom's Level:** Application

You are operating a drone adjacent to Sylvan Lake, which is in the Alberta – Red Deer Common Frequency Area. Traditional traffic at 8,000ft MSL and below will be monitoring and transmitting on which frequency?

- ☐ a) 123.2 MHz
- ☐ b) 126.7 MHz
- ☐ c) 122.875 MHz
- ☐ d) 121.5 MHz

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: c)** 122.875 MHz. This is a discrete air-to-air frequency specific to the Alberta–Red Deer Common Frequency Area. Students must be able to find area-specific frequencies in the Canada Flight Supplement (CFS), specifically the "C31 Areas with Discrete Air-to-Air Frequencies" section.

**Why this matters:** Different areas of Canada have different common frequencies for VFR traffic. If you are monitoring the wrong frequency, you miss traffic calls from aircraft that could conflict with your BVLOS operation. Always look up the specific frequency for your operating area in the CFS before every mission.

**Reference:** Canada Flight Supplement, "C31 Areas with Discrete Air-to-Air Frequencies Alberta-Red Deer Common Frequency Area" (NAV CANADA, 2026); L1C08 Radiotelephony

</details>

## Question 11
**Category:** L1C08 Radiotelephony | **Bloom's Level:** Recall

Pilots operating VFR en route in uncontrolled airspace when not communicating on an MF or ATF should continuously monitor which frequency?

- ☐ a) 121.5 MHz
- ☐ b) 123.2 MHz
- ☐ c) 126.7 MHz
- ☐ d) 122.2 MHz

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: c)** 126.7 MHz. This is the standard VFR en route frequency for uncontrolled airspace across Canada. For L1C BVLOS operations in Class G airspace, monitoring 126.7 MHz provides awareness of VFR traffic transiting through or near your operating area. Broadcasting your own RPAS operations on this frequency alerts manned traffic to your presence.

**Reference:** TC AIM — TP 14371; L1C08 Radiotelephony — Key Frequencies for L1C Operations
