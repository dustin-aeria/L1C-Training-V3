<summary>Instructor Notes — Module Purpose</summary>

This is the **integration module** — everything learned in Air Law, Systems, Human Factors, Meteorology, Navigation, and Theory of Flight converges here into practical mission planning and execution. Students should leave this module able to **plan a complete BVLOS operation from start to finish**, not just understand the concepts.

**Teaching approach:** This module is process-driven. Walk students through the complete 7-phase planning process using the Squamish Valley worked example. By the end of this session, every student should have:
1. Understood the complete flight planning process
2. Worked through a realistic multi-segment scenario
3. Begun planning their own flight review scenario

**The key shift:** Previous modules taught WHAT to know. This module teaches HOW TO DO. Every section should answer: "What do I actually produce? What tools do I use? What decisions do I make?"

**Critical teaching point:** The Squamish Valley scenario is deliberately complex — it involves transitions between L1C and Advanced operations, multiple population density zones, aerodrome proximity considerations, and real-world obstacles. This complexity is intentional. Students who can plan THIS operation can plan ANY L1C operation.

## LEARNING OBJECTIVES

After completing this module, you will be able to:

1. Execute the complete 7-phase BVLOS mission planning process
2. Determine L1C eligibility for multi-segment routes with varying population densities
3. Identify when operations transition between L1C and Advanced categories
4. Calculate operational volumes using actual aircraft performance data
5. Analyze terrain and identify obstacles along a planned route
6. Develop DAA strategies including VO positioning optimization
7. Calculate flight time including wind effects and determine point-of-no-return
8. Produce a complete Flight Plan Package suitable for flight review
9. Brief crews and execute go/no-go decisions
10. Document operations and report incidents appropriately

## SECTION 1: The Flight Planning Process Overview

### 1.1 The 7 Phases of BVLOS Mission Planning

Every BVLOS operation follows the same fundamental planning process. The phases build on each other — you cannot skip ahead.

| Phase | Timing | Focus | Key Activities |
|-------|--------|-------|----------------|
| **1** | 72+ hours before | Client Engagement & Feasibility | Can we do this? Initial L1C eligibility screening |
| **2** | 48-72 hours before | Regulatory Compliance | Detailed airspace, population, aerodrome analysis |
| **3** | 24-48 hours before | Route & Operational Volume | Design route, calculate operational volume |
| **4** | 24-48 hours before | Performance & Endurance | Flight time, PNR, battery/fuel requirements |
| **5** | 24 hours before | Risk Assessment & Mitigation | DAA strategy, VO placement, emergency planning |
| **6** | 24 hours before | Crew & Equipment | Crew assignments, equipment prep, logistics |
| **7** | Day of operation | Execution | Weather check, go/no-go, briefing, fly, debrief |

## SECTION 2: Phase 1 — Client Engagement & Initial Feasibility

### 2.1 The Intake Conversation

Before any detailed planning, you need to understand what the client wants and quickly assess whether it's feasible under L1C.

**Essential questions to ask:**

| Question | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------------|
| What is the objective? | Defines mission profile (survey, inspection, mapping) |
| Where exactly? | Coordinates or area definition for regulatory analysis |
| When does it need to happen? | Timeline feasibility, seasonal considerations |
| What data/deliverable do they need? | Determines payload, flight profile, coverage requirements |
| Are there any access restrictions? | Land access, permits, client site rules |
| What's the budget? | Determines crew size, equipment options |

### 2.2 Quick L1C Eligibility Screening

Before investing time in detailed planning, do a rapid feasibility check:

```
QUICK L1C FEASIBILITY SCREEN

□ Is the location likely Class G airspace?
  → Check NAV Drone or VNC chart for obvious controlled airspace
  → If clearly in controlled airspace → NOT L1C (stop here)

□ Is the location clearly >5 NM from any airport?
  → Quick check against known airports
  → If obviously near a major airport → NOT L1C (stop here)

□ Is the location over obviously populated area (town, city)?
  → Visual assessment from satellite imagery
  → If over dense urban area → NOT L1C (stop here)

□ Is the RPA appropriate?
  → Is it small or medium RPA with appropriate declaration?
  → If no declaration or >150 kg → NOT L1C (stop here)

PASSED QUICK SCREEN?
→ YES: Proceed to detailed Phase 2 analysis
→ NO: Discuss SFOC options with client or decline
```

### 2.3 Initial Scope Document

If the quick screen passes, document the initial scope:

- Client name and contact
- Mission objective (one sentence)
- General location (coordinates or area description)
- Approximate route length or area size
- Proposed timeline
- Initial equipment/crew estimate
- Identified concerns requiring detailed analysis
- Next steps and timeline for detailed planning

### 2.4 Go/No-Go Gate 1

**Gate 1 Question:** Is this operation POTENTIALLY feasible under L1C?

- If YES → Proceed to Phase 2 (detailed analysis)
- If NO → Discuss SFOC requirements or decline
- If UNCERTAIN → Proceed to Phase 2 to determine

## SECTION 3: Phase 2 — Regulatory Compliance Verification

This is where you do the detailed analysis to confirm L1C eligibility and identify any segments that may require different operational categories.

### 3.1 Airspace Determination Workflow

**Step 1: Identify the operating area on a VNC chart**
- Locate your route/area on the VFR Navigation Chart
- Identify any controlled airspace boundaries (Class C, D, E)
- Note any CYA (advisory) or CYR (restricted) areas

**Step 2: Verify in NAV Drone**
- Enter your route coordinates
- Confirm airspace classification
- Check for any temporary restrictions or NOTAMs

**Step 3: Check the Designated Airspace Handbook**
- Verify exact boundaries of any nearby controlled airspace
- Check effective times (some airspace is only active certain hours)
- Identify user agencies for any CYR areas

**Step 4: Document your findings**
- Screenshot the NAV Drone display
- Note the airspace classification for each segment of your route
- Identify any areas requiring authorization

### 3.2 Population Density Route Analysis

For L1C operations, you must analyze population density along the ENTIRE route, not just at a single point.

**Step 1: Define your operational volume corridor**
- Your operational volume extends beyond the flight path
- Use minimum 500m buffer on each side (1 km total corridor)
- This is the area you need to analyze

**Step 2: Identify all census subdivisions (CSDs) your corridor crosses**
- Use DSST-2 or Statistics Canada GeoCoder
- List every CSD the operational volume touches

**Step 3: Determine population density for each CSD**
- Access Statistics Canada Dissemination Area data
- Record population density (pp/km²) for each area
- **Use the HIGHEST density value** — that determines your pathway

**Step 4: Classify each segment**

| Population Density | Classification | L1C Pathway | Declaration Required |
|-------------------|----------------|-------------|---------------------|
| ≤5 pp/km² | Unpopulated | 901.87(a) | SAD |
| >5 to ≤25 pp/km² | Sparsely Populated | 901.87(b) — small RPA only | PVD |
| >25 pp/km² | Populated | NOT L1C | SFOC required |

**Step 5: Check distance from populated areas**
- Even if operating over unpopulated area, must be ≥1 km from populated area (>25 pp/km²) for 901.87(a)
- If <1 km from populated area, need 901.87(b) pathway

### 3.3 Multi-Category Operation Planning

Many real-world routes cross multiple regulatory zones. You may need to operate under DIFFERENT categories for different segments.

**Scenario types:**

| Situation | How to Handle |
|-----------|--------------|
| Route crosses from unpopulated to sparsely populated | Entire route must use 901.87(b) if any segment is sparsely populated, OR split into separate operations |
| Route approaches within 1 km of populated area | That segment requires 901.87(b) even if over unpopulated |
| Route segment enters controlled airspace | That segment requires Advanced + NAV CANADA authorization |
| Route segment comes within 5 NM of aerodrome | That segment requires SFOC (not L1C eligible) |

**Designing for compliance:**
- Can the route be modified to avoid higher-restriction areas?
- Can the operation be split into multiple segments with different categories?
- Where do operational category transitions occur?
- What enables each category? (e.g., VO placement for EVLOS portions)

### 3.4 Aerodrome Distance Verification

**Step 1: Identify all aerodromes near your route**
- Check CFS for registered aerodromes
- Check CWAS for water aerodromes
- Note: unregistered airstrips may exist but don't trigger the 5 NM rule (though they still create air risk)

**Step 2: Calculate distances**
- Measure from EVERY point on your operational volume to the nearest aerodrome reference point
- The closest point of your operational volume matters, not just the flight path centerline
- Remember: operational volume includes your buffer

**Step 3: Document compliance**
- If ALL points are >5 NM → L1C eligible (for this factor)
- If ANY point is ≤5 NM → NOT L1C eligible, requires SFOC

## SECTION 4: Phase 3 — Route & Operational Volume Planning

### 4.1 Route Design Principles

Design your route to optimize for:
1. **Regulatory compliance** — Stay within L1C-eligible areas where possible
2. **Safety** — Avoid obstacles, minimize risk exposure
3. **Efficiency** — Minimize flight time while achieving mission objectives
4. **DAA capability** — Enable effective VO coverage
5. **Emergency options** — Maintain access to alternate landing sites

**Route design considerations:**

| Factor | Design Implication |
|--------|-------------------|
| Population density boundaries | Route around higher-density areas if possible |
| Distance from populated areas | Maintain ≥1 km buffer from >25 pp/km² areas |
| Terrain | Follow valleys for better C2 link; avoid terrain that blocks signals |
| Obstacles | Route away from towers, wires, and other vertical hazards |
| Wind | Align route with prevailing wind for efficiency on return leg |
| Sun position | Consider DAA implications for morning/evening operations |
| VO coverage | Ensure route stays within effective VO range |

### 4.2 Operational Volume Calculation — The Process

The operational volume calculation from your earlier modules (L1C06 original content) provides the MATH. Here's the PROCESS for gathering the inputs.

**Step 1: Gather your aircraft specifications**

From your aircraft manual, find:
- Navigation accuracy (GPS CEP or similar)
- Altitude accuracy (barometric altimeter spec)
- Maximum airspeed
- C2 link latency
- Lost-link behavior (loiter radius, RTH parameters)

**Step 2: Determine path definition errors**

From your mapping/terrain data sources:
- What is the resolution of your terrain data? (e.g., NRCAN MRDEM ±2m)
- What coordinate system are you using? Any conversion errors?
- What is the accuracy of your planned waypoints?

**Step 3: Calculate flight technical error**

- Navigation accuracy: ±___ m (from aircraft specs)
- Altitude accuracy: ±___ m (from aircraft specs)
- Latency contribution: (C2 latency in seconds) × (max airspeed in m/s) = ±___ m

**Step 4: Calculate contingency volume**

- Loiter radius: ___ m (from lost-link programming)
- RTH path deviation: ___ m (typically minimal for straight-line RTH)
- Pilot takeover distance: (12.5 seconds reaction time) × (max airspeed) = ___ m

**Step 5: Calculate ground risk buffer**

- Operating altitude AGL × buffer ratio (typically 1:1 for small RPA)
- Example: 400 ft AGL × 1:1 = 400 ft = 122 m horizontal buffer

**Step 6: Sum all components**

![Flight Geography Components](/images/flight-ops/Flgiht GEogrpahy.png)

```
FLIGHT GEOGRAPHY (one side)
  Path definition error:        ±___ m
  Flight technical error:       ±___ m
  Latency contribution:         ±___ m
  SUBTOTAL:                     ±___ m

CONTINGENCY VOLUME (one side)
  Loiter radius:                +___ m
  RTH deviation:                +___ m
  Pilot takeover:               +___ m
  SUBTOTAL:                     +___ m

GROUND RISK BUFFER (one side)
  Altitude × ratio:             +___ m

TOTAL ONE SIDE:                 ___ m
TOTAL CORRIDOR WIDTH:           ___ m (× 2)
```

### 4.3 Terrain Analysis Workflow

**Step 1: Obtain terrain data**
- Download elevation data for your route area
- Options: NRCAN CDEM, Google Earth Pro, commercial DEM providers
- Resolution matters — higher resolution = better accuracy

**Step 2: Create elevation profile**
- Plot elevation along your route centerline
- Identify highest and lowest points
- Calculate elevation change throughout flight

**Step 3: Determine AGL altitude requirements**
- Your declared maximum is 400 ft AGL
- Terrain-following requires knowing ground elevation at every point
- Calculate required MSL altitude: Ground elevation + AGL = MSL

**Step 4: Identify terrain challenges**
- Valleys that may block C2 link
- Ridges that require altitude changes
- Areas where maintaining 400 ft AGL is difficult

### 4.4 Obstacle Identification Workflow

**Step 1: Check aeronautical charts (VNC)**
- Identify charted obstacles (towers, masts)
- Note heights (AGL and ASL)

**Step 2: Search NOTAMs**
- Current NOTAMs may identify temporary obstacles
- Construction cranes, temporary towers, cables
- **Critical:** Look for obstacles with "non-standard marking" notes

**Step 3: Review satellite imagery**
- Identify structures not shown on charts
- Power lines (often visible as clearings through forest)
- Communications towers
- Wind turbines

**Step 4: Document all obstacles**
- Create obstacle list with: type, location, height (AGL and ASL), source
- Verify your planned altitude provides adequate clearance
- Consider obstacles in your operational volume, not just flight path

### 4.5 Creating the Route Documentation

**Produce these deliverables:**

1. **Route Map** — KML/KMZ file showing:
   - Flight path centerline
   - Operational volume boundaries
   - VO positions
   - Launch and recovery points
   - Key waypoints

2. **Operational Volume Calculation Worksheet** — Completed with your aircraft's actual values

3. **Terrain Analysis** — Elevation profile with:
   - Ground elevation along route
   - Planned flight altitude (MSL and AGL)
   - Clearance above terrain

4. **Obstacle List** — Table of all identified obstacles with clearance verification

### 5.2 Flight Time Calculation with Wind

Wind significantly affects flight time. ALWAYS calculate with wind, not just distance/airspeed.

**The Formula:**
- **Ground Speed** = Airspeed ± Wind Component
- **Leg Time** = Distance ÷ Ground Speed

**For headwind:** Ground Speed = Airspeed - Wind Speed
**For tailwind:** Ground Speed = Airspeed + Wind Speed

**Worked calculation structure:**

| Leg | Direction | Distance | Wind Effect | Ground Speed | Time |
|-----|-----------|----------|-------------|--------------|------|
| Outbound | [heading] | ___ m | [head/tail] ___ m/s | ___ m/s | ___ min |
| On-site | N/A | N/A | N/A | N/A | ___ min |
| Inbound | [heading] | ___ m | [head/tail] ___ m/s | ___ m/s | ___ min |
| **TOTAL** | | | | | **___ min** |

> **Critical Lesson from L1C04 Meteorology:** "Where possible, plan a return to home that will be with a tailwind to avoid remaining flight time miscalculations." A headwind on return can TRIPLE your return time compared to the outbound leg.

### 5.3 Point-of-No-Return (PNR) Calculation

The PNR is the point beyond which you cannot return to your launch point with remaining battery/fuel.

**Simple PNR Calculation:**

```
Available flight time = Total endurance - Reserve requirement
Reserve requirement = Typically 20-30% of total endurance

If return leg has headwind:
  Return time = Return distance ÷ (Airspeed - Wind)

PNR = Point where remaining flight time = Return time + Reserve
```

**For linear routes:**
- Calculate time to reach each waypoint
- Calculate time to return from each waypoint (including wind)
- PNR is where return time equals remaining time minus reserve

### 5.4 Battery/Fuel Planning

**Battery considerations:**
- Capacity degrades with age and cycle count
- Cold temperatures reduce effective capacity (up to 30% loss)
- High discharge rates reduce effective capacity
- Plan for worst-case, not nominal capacity

**Planning calculation:**

```
Nominal capacity:                    ___ min
Age/cycle degradation factor:        × ___% (e.g., 90% for well-used battery)
Temperature factor:                  × ___% (e.g., 85% for cold conditions)
Adjusted capacity:                   ___ min

Mission requirement:                 ___ min
Reserve (20-30%):                    ___ min
Total required:                      ___ min

MARGIN: Adjusted capacity - Total required = ___ min
(Must be positive!)
```

|--------|------------|------------|

### 6.2 Air Risk Assessment

**Factors to assess:**

| Factor | Assessment | Mitigation |
|--------|------------|------------|
| Airspace class | Class G confirmed | Operating in uncontrolled airspace |
| Proximity to aerodromes | ___ NM from nearest | Distance compliance |
| Known traffic | Agricultural aviation? Helicopters? Training area? | Timing, DAA, NOTAMs |
| Altitude | ≤400 ft AGL | Altitude limit compliance |

### 6.3 DAA Strategy Selection

Based on your route characteristics, select your DAA approach:

| Route Characteristic | Recommended DAA Strategy |
|---------------------|-------------------------|
| Linear route <4 NM total | Single VO at midpoint |
| Linear route 4-10 NM | VO network (VOs every ~3.5 km) |
| Linear route >10 NM | VO network OR technical DAA |
| Area coverage <12 km² | VOs at perimeter |
| Area coverage >12 km² | Technical DAA or extensive VO team |

### 6.4 VO Positioning Optimization

**Positioning principles:**
- Each VO must maintain visual contact with the AIRSPACE (not necessarily the aircraft)
- RPA must remain within 2 NM of at least one VO (per Standard 922.10(a))
- VOs need clear sightlines — avoid positioning behind terrain or vegetation
- VOs need reliable communication with pilot
- Consider sun position — don't position VOs looking into sun

**VO positioning checklist:**
- [ ] Each segment covered by at least one VO
- [ ] VO positions have clear sightlines
- [ ] VO positions accessible (can crew get there?)
- [ ] Communication verified from each VO position
- [ ] Sun position considered for time of operation
- [ ] Overlapping coverage where possible

### 6.5 Sun Position for Standard 923 Compliance

If using vision-based DAA under Standard 923, verify sun position compliance:

**Requirements:**
- Sun elevation ≥45° above horizon, OR
- RPA operating outside the 90° quadrant centered on sun azimuth

**How to check:**
1. Determine operation date and time
2. Use a sun position calculator (many free online tools)
3. Get sun elevation and azimuth for your location/time
4. If elevation <45°, identify the 90° quadrant to avoid
5. Design VO positions and route to comply

**Example:**
- Sun at 15° elevation, 135° azimuth (SE)
- Quadrant to avoid: 90°-180° (E to S)
- VOs should NOT look into this quadrant
- Route segments with RPA in this quadrant from VO perspective require caution

### 6.6 Emergency Response Planning

For each segment of your route, identify:

**Emergency landing sites:**
- Primary site (preferred)
- Alternate site(s)
- Criteria: flat, clear of people/property, accessible for recovery

**Emergency contacts:**
- ATC facility and frequency (if fly-away could enter controlled airspace)
- Client contact
- Emergency services
- Company operations center

**Emergency procedures by location:**
- What do you do if emergency occurs at each point along route?
- Where does the aircraft go?
- How do you notify relevant parties?

### 9.1 TSB Reporting Requirements

Per TC AIM RPA 3.2.37, certain events must be reported to the Transportation Safety Board:

**Reportable events:**
- Injury to any person
- Significant damage to property
- Collision or near-collision with manned aircraft
- Fly-away into controlled airspace
- Loss of control resulting in unintended impact
- Events revealing safety deficiencies in aircraft or procedures

**How to report:**
- TSB 24-hour hotline: 1-819-997-7887
- Online reporting via TSB website
- Include: date, time, location, aircraft info, description of event, actions taken

### 9.2 Service Difficulty Reporting

If your aircraft experiences a malfunction that could affect flight safety, report to Transport Canada:
- Service Difficulty Report (SDR) via Drone Management Portal
- Include: aircraft make/model/serial, description of issue, flight conditions

Regardless of external reporting requirements, document all anomalies internally:
- Date/time
- Personnel involved
- Description of event
- Immediate actions taken
- Root cause analysis (if possible)
- Corrective actions
- Follow-up required

## SECTION 10: Complete Worked Example — Squamish Valley Survey

This section walks through a complete BVLOS mission planning exercise using a real-world scenario. This is the type of comprehensive planning you'll demonstrate during your flight review.

### 10.1 The Scenario

**Client request:** "We need a BVLOS aerial survey of a corridor in the Squamish Valley, BC, from approximately Brackendale to near the Squamish Valley Campground area."

**Route coordinates:**
- Start point: 49.8914809° N, -123.2661479° W (northwestern end)
- End point: 49.8347706° N, -123.2125212° W (southeastern end)
- Total route length: Approximately 7.1 km

**Aircraft:** DJI Matrice 300 RTK or similar (small RPA, <25 kg)
- Airspeed: 15 m/s cruise
- Endurance: 40 minutes (with payload, adjusted)
- Declaration: SAD covering 922.08(1,2), 922.09-922.11

### 10.2 Phase 1: Initial Feasibility

**Quick L1C screening:**
- Location appears to be rural valley — likely Class G ✓
- No obvious major airports immediately adjacent ✓
- Area appears to be low population density ✓
- Small RPA with SAD — platform appropriate ✓

**Result:** Passes quick screen → proceed to detailed analysis

### 10.3 Phase 2: Regulatory Analysis

**Airspace determination:**
- NAV Drone analysis: Class G airspace throughout route
- Nearest controlled airspace: CYSE (Squamish Municipal) — need to verify distance
- No CYR areas affecting route
- **Result:** Airspace compliant for L1C

**Aerodrome proximity:**
- Squamish Municipal Airport (CYSE): N49°46'54", W123°09'43"
- Distance from route: Approximately 4-6 NM depending on segment
- **CRITICAL:** Southern portion of route approaches closer to CYSE
- Need to verify ALL points of operational volume are >5 NM
- If any segment is ≤5 NM → that segment requires SFOC, not L1C

**Population density analysis:**

The route crosses multiple census subdivisions:

| Segment | CSD Name | Population | Area (km²) | Density (pp/km²) | Classification |
|---------|----------|------------|------------|------------------|----------------|
| Leg 1 | Cheakamus 11, IRI | 95 | 18.815 | **5.049** | Sparsely Populated |
| Leg 2 | Squamish-Lillooet D, RDA | 1,057 | 3,040.202 | **0.348** | Unpopulated |

**Analysis:**
- Leg 1 density (5.049 pp/km²) is JUST over the 5 pp/km² threshold
- This makes Leg 1 "sparsely populated" — requires 901.87(b) with PVD
- Leg 2 is clearly unpopulated — qualifies for 901.87(a) with SAD

**The complication:**
- If using SAD (901.87(a) declaration), we CANNOT fly over Leg 1 (sparsely populated)
- If we have PVD (901.87(b) declaration), we CAN fly the entire route
- With only SAD, we would need to:
  - Operate Leg 2 as L1C BVLOS
  - Operate Leg 1 as Advanced EVLOS (with VO visual contact maintained)

**Distance from populated areas:**
- Squamish town (>25 pp/km²) is to the south/southeast
- Need to verify ≥1 km separation for 901.87(a) portions
- Southern end of route approaches town — verify buffer distance

### 10.4 Multi-Category Operation Design

Given a SAD declaration (922.08(1,2) + 922.09-922.11), the operation requires:

```
ROUTE SEGMENT ANALYSIS

SEGMENT 1 (Northwestern portion - over Squamish-Lillooet D)
├─ Population density: 0.348 pp/km² (unpopulated)
├─ Distance from populated area: >1 km (verify)
├─ Operational category: L1C BVLOS under 901.87(a)
├─ Declaration: SAD acceptable
└─ DAA: VO network or vision-based per Standard 923

TRANSITION POINT (where route crosses into Cheakamus 11)
├─ Must transition operational category here
└─ VO must be positioned to enable EVLOS

SEGMENT 2 (Southeastern portion - over Cheakamus 11)
├─ Population density: 5.049 pp/km² (sparsely populated)
├─ Operational category: NOT L1C with SAD
├─ Options:
│   ├─ OPTION A: Upgrade to PVD → operate as L1C 901.87(b)
│   ├─ OPTION B: Operate as Advanced EVLOS (VO maintains airspace contact)
│   └─ OPTION C: Terminate L1C portion before crossing into this area
└─ If EVLOS: VO must be within 2 NM and maintain airspace visual contact

APPROACH TO SQUAMISH (if route continues toward town)
├─ May enter within 1 km of populated area
├─ May enter within 5 NM of CYSE
└─ Would require SFOC or different operational approach
```

### 10.5 Phase 3: Route & Operational Volume

**Route design decisions:**
- Primary route follows valley floor
- Operational volume: 500m buffer each side (1 km corridor)
- Maximum altitude: 400 ft AGL (122 m)
- Terrain-following may be required due to valley terrain variation

**Operational volume calculation (for DJI M300 RTK):**

| Component | Value | Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| **Path Definition Error** | | |
| Map/terrain data | ±2 m | NRCAN MRDEM |
| Coordinate conversion | ±1 m | NAD83 to WGS84 |
| **Flight Technical Error** | | |
| Navigation accuracy | ±1 m | RTK-enabled GPS |
| Altitude accuracy | ±0.5 m | Barometric + GPS |
| Latency (200ms × 15 m/s) | ±3 m | |
| **Contingency Volume** | | |
| Hover/loiter radius | +25 m | M300 hover capability |
| Pilot takeover (12.5s × 15 m/s) | +188 m | |
| **Ground Risk Buffer** | | |
| 400 ft AGL × 1:1 | +122 m | |
| **TOTAL ONE SIDE** | **~342 m** | |
| **TOTAL CORRIDOR WIDTH** | **~684 m** | |

**Result:** Operational volume corridor is approximately 684 m wide — within our 1 km planned buffer.

![Operational Volume Example - Squamish Valley](/images/flight-ops/operational volume example.png)

**Terrain analysis:**
- Elevation profile shows variation along route
- Valley floor ranges from approximately 20-60 m ASL
- Maintaining 400 ft AGL requires adjusting MSL altitude
- Example: At 40 m ground elevation, fly at 162 m MSL (40 + 122)

**Obstacles identified:**
- Cable crossing near CYSE (from NOTAM I6376/25):
  - Location: 49°47'31"N, 123°09'58"W
  - Height: 80 ft AGL / 230 ft AMSL
  - Note: "MARKER BALLS MISSING" — non-standard marking
  - Status: Active through March 2026
- This obstacle is near CYSE, may affect southern portions if route extends that far

### 10.6 Phase 4: Performance Planning

**Flight time calculation:**

Assumptions:
- Aircraft airspeed: 15 m/s
- Forecast wind: 5 m/s from SE (135°)
- Route direction: SE to NW outbound

| Leg | Distance | Wind Effect | Ground Speed | Time |
|-----|----------|-------------|--------------|------|
| Outbound (NW to SE) | 7,100 m | Into headwind (partial) | ~11 m/s | 10.8 min |
| On-site (hover/survey) | — | — | — | 5.0 min |
| Inbound (SE to NW) | 7,100 m | With tailwind (partial) | ~19 m/s | 6.2 min |
| **TOTAL** | **14,200 m** | | | **22.0 min** |

**Battery planning:**
- Adjusted endurance (cold weather, aged battery): 34 min
- Flight time required: 22 min
- Reserve (25%): 8.5 min
- Total required: 30.5 min
- **Margin: 3.5 min** ✓ (acceptable but tight)

**Point of No Return:**
- With 34 min endurance and 25% reserve, available flight time = 25.5 min
- PNR occurs when return time = remaining flight time
- Estimated PNR: approximately 5.8 km from launch (at the 16-minute mark)
- Must begin return before reaching PNR

### 10.7 Phase 5: Risk & DAA Strategy

**DAA strategy selection:**

Given the 7.1 km route length, VO network is required:
- Single VO cannot cover entire route (exceeds 2 NM ≈ 3.7 km)
- Minimum 2 VOs required

**VO positioning:**

```
VO PLACEMENT PLAN

VO1 Position: Near launch point (49.8914809°N, -123.2661479°W)
├─ Coverage: Launch through approximately 3.5 km point
├─ Responsibilities: Launch/recovery, northern route segment
└─ Can observe: L1C BVLOS segment (Squamish-Lillooet D)

VO2 Position: Mid-route point (approximately 49.86°N, -123.24°W)
├─ Coverage: 3.5 km point through end point
├─ Responsibilities: Southern route segment
└─ Can observe: EVLOS segment (Cheakamus 11) if needed for category transition

Overlap zone: Approximately 0.5 km overlap for handoff
```

**Sun position consideration:**
- For morning operation (e.g., 0930 local in February)
- Sun elevation: ~16° (low)
- Sun azimuth: ~135° (SE)
- **Impact:** Looking SE places observers looking toward sun
- **Mitigation:** VO2 should position to look NW (with sun behind), avoid looking SE

**Emergency response:**
- Primary alternate landing: Launch point
- Secondary alternate: Cleared area mid-route
- ATC contact if fly-away: Vancouver Centre (verify frequency)
- Nearest hospital: Squamish General

### 10.8 Phase 6: Crew & Briefing

**Crew assignment:**
- PIC: [Name] — L1C certified, recency current
- VO1: [Name] — Basic certified, VO training complete
- VO2: [Name] — Basic certified, VO training complete

**Communication plan:**
- VHF handheld radios: Channel [X]
- Check-in interval: At each waypoint or every 2 minutes
- Emergency code words:
  - "Traffic": VO sees aircraft in airspace
  - "Abort": Immediate RTH required
  - "Fly-away": Aircraft not responding, initiate emergency procedure
