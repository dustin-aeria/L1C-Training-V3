<details>
<summary>Instructor Notes — Module Purpose</summary>

This is the **demonstration module** — students have learned how to plan a BVLOS operation in L1C06; now they learn how to PRESENT and DEMONSTRATE that competence to a flight reviewer. The focus shifts from "how to do it" to "how to show that you can do it."

**Teaching approach:** The flight review is a professional assessment. Treat this module as preparation for a job interview where the "interview questions" are the 8 assessed exercises. Students should practice presenting their work, explaining their decisions, and demonstrating crew coordination.

**Critical point:** Students will receive a flight review location from the instructor (or will plan their OWN scenario for the actual flight review). The scenario may involve multi-category operation — transitions between L1C and Advanced based on population density, airspace, or VO coverage. Students must be able to EXPLAIN why each segment operates under which category.

**The key shift:** L1C06 taught HOW to plan. This module teaches HOW TO SHOW that you can plan. The flight reviewer is assessing not just the plan, but your understanding of WHY you planned it that way.

</details>

## LEARNING OBJECTIVES

After completing this module, you will be able to:

1. Describe the complete L1C certification pathway and flight review structure
2. Present a site survey as a verbal briefing with supporting documentation
3. Explain regulatory transitions between L1C and Advanced operation categories
4. Walk through mission planning calculations and justify your decisions
5. Brief emergency procedures with proper VO coordination
6. Demonstrate CRM communication during traffic detection scenarios
7. Perform pre-flight checks systematically
8. Execute take-off, navigation, and landing to flight review standards
9. Avoid common flight review failures

## SECTION 1: The Flight Review Framework

### 1.1 L1C Certification Pathway

The certification pathway consists of sequential steps that must be completed in order:

```
18+ years old
     │
     ▼
Advanced Exam Pass
     │
     ▼
20-Hour L1C Ground School (TP 15530) ← You are here
     │
     ▼
L1C Written Exam
     │
     ▼
In-Person Flight Review ← This module prepares you for this
     │
     ▼
L1C RPAS Pilot Certificate
```

### 1.2 What the Flight Review Assesses

The flight review is a **holistic operational assessment** conducted by a qualified flight reviewer who holds an L1C certificate with Flight Reviewer rating.

**The review assesses whether you can:**
- PLAN a BVLOS operation (demonstrated through your Flight Plan Package)
- EXPLAIN your planning decisions (verbal presentation and Q&A)
- EXECUTE the operation safely (flight demonstration)
- MANAGE a crew (VO coordination and CRM)
- RESPOND to emergencies (procedure demonstration)

### 1.3 The 8 Assessed Exercises

Per Standard 921.07, the flight review consists of 8 exercises:

| Exercise | Type | What's Being Assessed |
|----------|------|----------------------|
| 1. Site Survey | Ground | Can you systematically assess an operating area? |
| 2. Mission Planning | Ground | Can you plan a complete BVLOS operation? |
| 3. Emergency Procedures | Ground | Do you know what to do when things go wrong? |
| 4. Pre-Flight Checks | Ground | Can you systematically verify aircraft readiness? |
| 5. Take-Off | Flight | Can you safely launch the aircraft? |
| 6. Obstacle Navigation | Flight | Can you navigate using GCS and visual references? |
| 7. Distance Recognition | Flight | Can you judge distance from obstacles/boundaries? |
| 8. Landing | Flight | Can you safely recover the aircraft? |

### 1.4 Pass/Fail Criteria

**Each exercise is pass/fail.** If ANY exercise does not meet requirements, the entire flight review is failed.

**Automatic failure triggers:**
- Unsafe flying or behavior
- Incomplete or inadequate site survey
- Lack of training or competency in any area
- Not using effective scanning techniques (for VOs)
- Inability to explain regulatory requirements
- Poor CRM communication

**After failure:** May reattempt after 24 hours

**After success:** Flight reviewer enters information in Drone Management Portal within 24 hours. You're notified and can apply for your certificate.

Organize your Flight Plan Package in a logical order that follows your verbal presentation:


## SECTION 3: Exercise 1 — Presenting Your Site Survey

### 3.1 What the Reviewer is Looking For

The site survey assessment verifies you can:
- Systematically assess all required factors per CARs 901.27
- Use appropriate tools and data sources
- Document your findings clearly
- Identify hazards and constraints
- Make informed go/no-go recommendations

### 3.2 Structuring Your Site Survey Presentation

Present your site survey as a professional briefing, covering each required factor:

**Opening:**
> "I'll walk you through my site survey for [location]. I've assessed all factors required under CARs 901.27, and I'll cover airspace, aerodromes, population density, terrain, obstacles, and weather."

**Airspace:**
> "The operating area is in Class G uncontrolled airspace. I verified this using [NAV Drone/VNC chart/DAH]. [Show documentation.] There are no CYR restricted areas affecting the operational volume. The nearest controlled airspace is [describe], which is [distance] away."

**Aerodromes:**
> "The nearest CFS-listed aerodrome is [name] at [distance] from the nearest point of my operational volume. I measured this using [DSST-2/direct calculation]. [Show documentation.] This exceeds the 5 NM requirement for L1C operations."

**Population Density:**
> "My route crosses [number] census subdivisions. [Show population density analysis.] The highest density along the route is [X] pp/km² in [CSD name], which classifies as [unpopulated/sparsely populated]. This means my operation falls under [901.87(a)/901.87(b)]."

**If your route involves regulatory transitions:**
> "I should note that my route crosses from [CSD A] at [X] pp/km² to [CSD B] at [Y] pp/km². This means [explain the regulatory implication]. I've designed the operation to [explain how you handle it]."

**Terrain:**
> "The terrain along the route varies from [X] to [Y] metres elevation. [Show elevation profile.] To maintain 400 ft AGL throughout, I'll need to adjust my MSL altitude as follows: [explain]. The most challenging terrain feature is [describe]."

**Obstacles:**
> "I've identified [number] obstacles along or near the route. [Go through obstacle list.] The most significant is [describe], which is [height] AGL at [location]. My planned altitude provides [X] metres clearance. I found this obstacle through [NOTAM/chart/satellite imagery]."

**Weather:**
> "Current weather at [reference station] is [summarize METAR]. The forecast shows [summarize TAF]. This is [within/outside] my operational limits for [wind/visibility/etc.]."

**Conclusion:**
> "Based on my site survey, this operation [is/is not] feasible under L1C. My primary concerns are [list any] and I've mitigated them by [explain]."

### 3.3 Questions the Reviewer May Ask

Be prepared to answer:

| Question | What They're Assessing |
|----------|----------------------|
| "How did you determine this is Class G airspace?" | Do you understand airspace classification? |
| "What if a NOTAM restricts this area tomorrow?" | Do you check NOTAMs before every flight? |
| "This CSD shows 5.049 pp/km². Why is that significant?" | Do you understand population density thresholds? |
| "What if you found this population density was actually 4.9?" | Do you understand how thresholds change your operation? |
| "How did you find that obstacle?" | Do you use multiple sources for obstacle identification? |
| "Is there anything not on the charts that might be a hazard?" | Do you think beyond published information? |

### 3.4 Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Vague sources:** "I checked online" — be specific about tools and data sources
- **Missing population density analysis:** Must document ALL CSDs along route
- **Single-point aerodrome check:** Check from EVERY point of operational volume, not just launch site
- **Outdated NOTAMs:** Must be checked within hours of flight, not days
- **Ignoring terrain:** Especially important for maintaining AGL altitude

## SECTION 4: Exercise 2 — Presenting Your Mission Plan

### 4.1 The Nine Flight Criteria

Your mission planning presentation should demonstrate all nine flight criteria:

1. Take-off and landing area location
2. Inspection/survey location(s)
3. Total distance RPA will fly
4. Forecast winds at cruising altitude
5. RPA airborne time
6. Critical point / point of no return
7. Horizontal buffer (operational volume) with population density and air risk
8. Anticipated elevation change
9. Visual observer placement

### 4.2 Structuring Your Mission Plan Presentation

**Route overview:**
> "My route begins at [coordinates], which is [describe location]. It proceeds [direction] for [distance] to [coordinates/description]. Total route distance is [X] km, or approximately [Y] NM."
>
> [Show route map with operational volume]

**Operational volume:**
> "I've calculated my operational volume using my aircraft's specifications. [Walk through calculation.] This results in a corridor width of [X] metres. I've used a minimum 500m buffer on each side for population density assessment."

**Population and regulatory analysis:**
> "Within this operational volume, the highest population density is [X] pp/km², making this a [classification]. Combined with my aircraft's [SAD/PVD] declaration, this operation falls under [901.87(a)/901.87(b)]."

**If multi-category operation:**
> "This route involves a transition between operational categories. From the launch point to approximately [waypoint/distance], the operation is L1C BVLOS under 901.87(a). At that point, the population density increases to [X] pp/km², requiring [explain — either PVD for 901.87(b), or Advanced EVLOS with VO coverage]."

**Performance calculations:**
> "With forecast winds of [speed] from [direction], my flight time calculation shows:
> - Outbound: [time] at [groundspeed]
> - On-site: [time]
> - Inbound: [time] at [groundspeed]
> - Total: [time]
>
> My aircraft's adjusted endurance is [X] minutes. With [Y]% reserve, I have [Z] minutes margin."

**Point of no return:**
> "My point of no return is at [distance/location], which I'll reach at approximately [time] into the flight. I must begin return before this point to ensure safe recovery with reserves."

**VO placement:**
> "I've positioned [number] visual observers as follows: [describe each position]. VO1 covers [segment] from [position]. VO2 covers [segment] from [position]. This ensures the RPA is always within 2 NM of at least one VO as required by Standard 922.10(a). The overlap zone is at [describe]."

### 4.3 Explaining Regulatory Transitions

If your scenario involves transitioning between L1C and Advanced operations, you MUST be able to explain clearly:

**Why the transition is required:**
> "The transition from L1C to Advanced is required because [specific reason]:
> - Population density exceeds threshold (>5 pp/km² for 901.87(a) SAD operation), OR
> - Within 1 km of populated area, OR
> - Entering airspace requiring authorization"

**What enables each category:**
> "The L1C BVLOS portion is enabled by:
> - Unpopulated area (≤5 pp/km²)
> - Class G airspace
> - >5 NM from aerodromes
> - SAD declaration covering 922.08-922.11
>
> The Advanced EVLOS portion is enabled by:
> - VO maintains visual contact with airspace (not BVLOS)
> - Aircraft within 2 NM of VO
> - Advanced pilot certificate
> - Appropriate declaration"

**How you manage the transition:**
> "The transition occurs at [location/waypoint]. At this point:
> 1. I announce 'Entering EVLOS segment'
> 2. VO confirms airspace visual contact established
> 3. Operation continues under Advanced EVLOS rules
> 4. Aircraft remains within 2 NM of VO"

### 4.4 Questions the Reviewer May Ask

| Question | What They're Assessing |
|----------|----------------------|
| "Walk me through your operational volume calculation" | Can you explain, not just recite? |
| "Why is PNR important for this flight?" | Do you understand the safety implication? |
| "What if the wind changes to [opposite direction]?" | Can you recalculate on the fly? |
| "Where exactly does your operation transition from L1C to Advanced?" | Do you understand the regulatory boundaries? |
| "What enables you to operate this segment as L1C?" | Do you understand the requirements? |
| "What would change if this area was 26 pp/km² instead of 24?" | Do you understand threshold implications? |

## SECTION 5: Exercise 3 — Demonstrating Emergency Procedures

### 5.1 What the Reviewer is Looking For

Emergency procedure assessment verifies you can:
- Articulate clear procedures for all seven CARs 901.23 categories
- Coordinate effectively with crew (especially VOs)
- Make quick decisions under pressure
- Communicate clearly and concisely

### 5.2 The Seven Emergency Categories

You must be able to brief and demonstrate procedures for:

1. **Control station failure**
2. **Equipment failure**
3. **Failure of the RPA**
4. **Loss of C2 link**
5. **Fly-away**
6. **Flight termination**
7. **Traffic detection and avoidance**

### 5.3 Emergency Procedure Presentation Format

For each emergency, use this structure:

**Recognition → Immediate Action → Crew Communication → Expected Outcome**

**Example: Loss of C2 Link**

> "LOSS OF C2 LINK PROCEDURE:
>
> **Recognition:** GCS shows link status red, no telemetry updates, commands not acknowledged.
>
> **Immediate Action:**
> 1. Announce 'Lost link' to crew
> 2. Do NOT attempt to send additional commands (may interfere with recovery)
> 3. Monitor for expected lost-link behavior
>
> **Crew Communication:**
> - 'VO, lost link, aircraft should be entering [hover/RTH/land] mode'
> - 'VO, confirm aircraft behavior'
>
> **Expected Outcome:**
> - Aircraft enters pre-programmed lost-link behavior: [describe your aircraft's specific behavior]
> - For my M300: Hovers for 30 seconds, then RTH at [altitude]
>
> **If aircraft NOT following expected behavior:**
> - This is a FLY-AWAY, not lost link
> - Transition to fly-away procedure"

### 5.4 Traffic Detection and Avoidance — The CRM Demonstration

The traffic DAA scenario is the most critical CRM exercise. You must demonstrate the complete sequence:

**Practice dialogue:**

> **VO:** "Traffic! Four o'clock, low, moving left to right, looks like a helicopter."
>
> **PIC:** "Traffic four o'clock, looking."
>
> [PIC assesses on GCS and visually if possible]
>
> **PIC:** "Traffic in sight. Descending and holding position. VO, call when clear."
>
> [PIC maneuvers aircraft — descend, hold, or move away from traffic]
>
> **VO:** "Traffic continuing east, no longer a factor."
>
> **PIC:** "Confirmed traffic clear. Resuming mission."

**Key elements the reviewer watches for:**
- VO uses clear, concise format (position, altitude, movement)
- PIC acknowledges immediately
- PIC takes positive action (not just "watching")
- Clear confirmation of resolution
- Return to normal operations only after confirmed clear

### 5.5 Fly-Away Procedure Demonstration

The fly-away procedure demonstrates your understanding of the most serious BVLOS emergency:

> "FLY-AWAY PROCEDURE:
>
> **Recognition:** Aircraft not responding to commands AND not following pre-programmed lost-link behavior. Position changing unpredictably, or aircraft moving in direction not commanded.
>
> **Distinction from lost link:** Lost link = aircraft follows expected behavior. Fly-away = aircraft NOT following expected behavior.
>
> **Immediate Action:**
> 1. Announce 'Fly-away, fly-away, fly-away' to all crew
> 2. Attempt control recovery for maximum 30 seconds
> 3. If no recovery, initiate ATC notification
>
> **ATC Notification (if aircraft may enter controlled airspace):**
> '[Facility], this is [identifier], drone fly-away in progress, position [coordinates], altitude approximately [feet], heading [direction], request traffic advisory.'
>
> **Information to provide:**
> - Your identifier
> - 'Drone fly-away'
> - Last known position
> - Last known altitude
> - Direction of travel
> - Estimated battery time remaining
> - Your contact number
>
> **Post fly-away:**
> - Remain available for ATC updates
> - Document everything
> - Determine reporting requirements (TSB, TC)
> - Secure aircraft if recovered"

## SECTION 6: Exercises 4-8 — The Flight Demonstration

### 6.1 Exercise 4: Pre-Flight Checks

**What the reviewer watches:**
- Systematic, not random
- Using a checklist (not memory)
- Actually checking items (not just reading)
- Verifying, not assuming

**Demonstrate systematic approach:**

> "I'm beginning pre-flight checks. I'll work through my aircraft checklist starting with airframe, then propulsion, control surfaces, payload, GCS, communications, and batteries."

Talk through each item as you check it:

> "Checking propellers... Propeller 1 secure, no chips or damage. Propeller 2 secure, no chips or damage..." [continue through all]

**Critical items to emphasize:**
- GPS lock and satellite count
- Battery voltage and capacity
- Compass calibration status
- C2 link status
- Geofence configuration
- Lost-link behavior setting
- Flight plan loaded correctly

### 6.2 Exercise 5: Take-Off

**What the reviewer watches:**
- Clear communication with crew
- Proper sequence (area check, power up, launch)
- Control demonstrated during ascent
- Stable hover established

**Demonstration sequence:**

> "VO, radio check."
> [VO confirms]
>
> "Beginning take-off sequence. Area check — VO confirm clear."
> [VO confirms]
>
> "Arming motors... Motors armed. Launching in three... two... one... launching."
> [Aircraft ascends]
>
> "Climbing through [altitude]... Establishing hover at [altitude]."
> [Demonstrate stable hover]
>
> "Hover established. Systems check: GPS [status], battery [percent], C2 link [status]. Proceeding on mission."

### 6.3 Exercise 6: Obstacle Navigation

**What the reviewer watches:**
- Smooth control inputs
- Awareness of position relative to obstacles
- Use of GCS telemetry for position awareness
- Ability to navigate when aircraft is beyond visual range

**Demonstrate:**
- Navigate to and around designated waypoints
- Maintain awareness using GCS display
- Announce position and intentions
- Show you can navigate by instruments when aircraft is distant

> "Approaching waypoint 2. GCS shows [distance] to waypoint. Turning to heading [XXX] for waypoint 3."

### 6.4 Exercise 7: Distance Recognition

**What the reviewer watches:**
- Accurate judgment of distance from obstacles
- Accurate judgment of distance from operational boundaries
- Use of both visual and GCS references
- Appropriate caution near limits

**Demonstrate:**
- Estimate distance to obstacles/boundaries
- Verify with GCS telemetry
- Maintain appropriate separation
- Know when you're approaching limits

> "I estimate we're approximately [X] metres from the tree line. GCS shows [Y] metres. Maintaining [Z] metre buffer."

### 6.5 Exercise 8: Landing

**What the reviewer watches:**
- Controlled descent
- Awareness of landing zone conditions
- Proper communication with VO
- Smooth touchdown
- Post-landing procedures

**Demonstration sequence:**

> "Beginning return to landing zone."
>
> "VO, landing zone check."
> [VO confirms clear]
>
> "Descending through [altitude]... On approach to landing zone."
>
> "Short final... Touchdown."
>
> "Aircraft on ground. Disarming motors... Motors disarmed. Aircraft secured."

## SECTION 7: Common Flight Review Failures and How to Avoid Them

### 7.1 Ground Portion Failures

| Failure Mode | Why It Happens | How to Avoid |
|--------------|----------------|--------------|
| **Incomplete site survey** | Didn't document all 901.27 factors | Use the checklist; have documentation for each factor |
| **Can't explain population density** | Memorized numbers without understanding | Know WHY each threshold matters |
| **Vague emergency procedures** | Didn't practice verbalizing | Practice saying procedures out loud, not just reading |
| **Can't explain regulatory transitions** | Doesn't understand L1C vs Advanced boundaries | Study Section 4.3 of this module |
| **Missing calculations** | Didn't show the work | Document calculations, not just answers |
| **Can't recalculate if conditions change** | Memorized specific scenario, not the method | Practice calculations with different values |

### 7.2 Flight Portion Failures

| Failure Mode | Why It Happens | How to Avoid |
|--------------|----------------|--------------|
| **Unsafe flying** | Lack of practice, overconfidence | Practice extensively before review |
| **Poor CRM communication** | Didn't practice with crew | Practice VO coordination repeatedly |
| **Hesitation during emergency scenario** | Didn't internalize procedures | Practice emergency procedures until automatic |
| **Loss of situational awareness** | Focused on one thing, lost big picture | Practice scanning pattern (aircraft, GCS, environment, crew) |
| **Can't judge distance** | Lack of experience | Practice estimating and verifying distances |

### 7.3 The Most Common Overall Failure

**"Knows the what, but not the why."**

The reviewer will probe your understanding. They may ask:
- "Why is that the threshold?"
- "What happens if...?"
- "Why did you choose this approach?"

If your answer is "because that's what the regulation says" without understanding the underlying reason, you may fail.

**Example of inadequate vs. adequate response:**

**Inadequate:** "The population density threshold is 5 pp/km² because that's what's in 901.87."

**Adequate:** "The 5 pp/km² threshold comes from SORA ground risk analysis. At this density, the probability of the aircraft striking a person is low enough that standard containment measures provide acceptable risk. Above 5 pp/km², we need enhanced reliability through a PVD to achieve the same risk level."

## SECTION 8: Practice Scenarios

### 8.1 Practice Scenario: Multi-Category Route

**Your task:** Plan and present an operation for a route that requires both L1C BVLOS and Advanced EVLOS segments.

**Scenario:** Survey route from Point A to Point B
- Point A coordinates: [given by instructor]
- Point B coordinates: [given by instructor]
- Aircraft: Small RPA with SAD (922.08(1,2) + 922.09-922.11)
- Route crosses from unpopulated area (2.1 pp/km²) to sparsely populated area (7.3 pp/km²)

**You must:**
1. Complete full site survey for entire route
2. Identify where the regulatory transition occurs
3. Explain what enables each segment
4. Position VOs to enable both segments
5. Plan the operational category transition
6. Calculate flight time and PNR
7. Prepare emergency procedures for each segment

**Self-assessment questions:**
- Can you point to the exact location where the transition occurs?
- Can you explain why you can't continue L1C BVLOS in the sparsely populated segment with only a SAD?
- What would need to change to operate the entire route as L1C?
- How do your VO positions enable the EVLOS segment?

### 8.2 Practice Scenario: Boundary Condition

**Scenario:** Your population density analysis shows one CSD at 4.95 pp/km² and an adjacent CSD at 5.05 pp/km².

**Questions to answer:**
1. How does this tiny difference (0.1 pp/km²) change your operation?
2. What declaration do you need for each segment?
3. If your route only briefly crosses the 5.05 CSD, can you modify your route to avoid it?
4. What sources did you use, and how confident are you in the accuracy?
5. What would you tell a client who says "it's basically the same"?

### 8.3 Practice Scenario: Emergency Response

**Scenario:** You're at minute 18 of a flight with 35-minute adjusted endurance. The aircraft is 5.2 km from the launch point. Suddenly, telemetry freezes and your command inputs have no effect. The aircraft continues moving northeast at approximately 12 m/s.

**Walk through:**
1. Is this lost link or fly-away? How do you know?
2. What is your immediate verbal announcement to crew?
3. The aircraft could reach controlled airspace (Class E starting at 700 ft AGL) in approximately 3 minutes. What do you do?
4. Write out the exact ATC notification you would make.
5. What's the maximum distance this aircraft could travel before battery exhaustion?

## SECTION 10: Discussion Prompts

### Full Walk-Through (30 minutes)
*"Present your complete Flight Plan Package as if I am the flight reviewer. Start with your site survey, walk through your mission plan, explain any regulatory transitions, brief your emergency procedures. I'll ask questions throughout."*

### The Transition Challenge (15 minutes)
*"Your route requires operating L1C BVLOS for 4 km, then transitioning to Advanced EVLOS for 2 km due to population density. Walk me through exactly how you'd brief this to a flight reviewer. Where is the transition point? What enables each segment? How do your VOs support both?"*

### The What-If Game (15 minutes)
*"Let's change one variable in your plan. What if: The wind shifts 180°? One VO is sick? You find a new obstacle during site assessment? The population density data shows a higher value than expected? Walk through how each change affects your plan."*

### Emergency Pressure Test (10 minutes)
*"I'm going to describe an in-flight emergency. You have 10 seconds to begin your response. Ready? 'Your VO just called traffic, 2 o'clock, same altitude, converging.' Go."*

**Q2.** If your route crosses from an area of 3 pp/km² to an area of 8 pp/km², and you have only an SAD declaration, what must you do?
<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer:** You cannot operate the 8 pp/km² segment as L1C BVLOS with only an SAD. Options: (1) obtain a PVD to enable 901.87(b), (2) operate that segment as Advanced EVLOS with VO maintaining airspace visual contact, or (3) modify the route to avoid the sparsely populated area.

</details>

**Q3.** During a traffic detection scenario, what is the proper communication sequence?
<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer:** VO reports traffic (position, altitude, movement) → PIC acknowledges ("Traffic [position], looking") → PIC takes action → VO calls when clear → PIC confirms clear and resumes mission.

</details>

**Q4.** What's the difference between lost C2 link and fly-away?
<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer:** Lost C2 link: link interrupted but aircraft follows pre-programmed lost-link behavior (predictable). Fly-away: link lost AND aircraft NOT following pre-programmed behavior (unpredictable, dangerous).

</details>

**Q5.** What documentation should you bring to your flight review?

## SECTION 12: Final Thoughts

### The Flight Review is a Demonstration, Not a Test

The flight review is your opportunity to demonstrate competence, not just answer questions correctly. Show the reviewer that you:

- **Think systematically** — Use processes and checklists, not random approaches
- **Understand the "why"** — Can explain the reasoning behind regulations and procedures
- **Communicate clearly** — CRM is as important as technical knowledge
- **Make safe decisions** — Know when to go and when to stop
- **Can adapt** — Can recalculate and adjust when conditions change

### Trust Your Preparation

If you've completed this ground school, practiced your presentations, and built a thorough Flight Plan Package, you're prepared. The flight review verifies what you've already learned.

### After the Flight Review

Upon successful completion:
- Flight reviewer enters information in DMP within 24 hours
- You receive email notification
- Log into DMP to pay for certificate issuance ($125)
- Receive your L1C RPAS Pilot Certificate

**You're now authorized to conduct Level 1 Complex BVLOS operations.**

But remember: the certificate is permission to operate, not proof of expertise. Continue learning, practicing, and improving. Every flight is an opportunity to become a better pilot.
