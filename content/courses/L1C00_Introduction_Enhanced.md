## SECTION 1: Welcome to Level 1 Complex Operations

### 1.1 What You Are Here to Achieve

You are training to become one of Canada's first generation of regulation-certified BVLOS drone pilots. Until November 2025, every beyond-visual-line-of-sight drone flight in Canada required a Special Flight Operations Certificate — a one-off approval from Transport Canada that could take weeks or months to process. The Level 1 Complex pilot certificate changes that entirely.

With an L1C certificate, you will be authorized to conduct routine BVLOS operations in defined conditions without needing individual TC approval for each mission. This is the single most significant expansion of RPAS privileges since Canada created Part IX of the Canadian Aviation Regulations in 2019.

**What L1C unlocks:**

* Beyond Visual Line of Sight (BVLOS) flights with small RPA (250 g – 25 kg) and medium RPA (25 kg – 150 kg)
* Operations in uncontrolled (Class G) airspace at or below 400 ft AGL
* Flights more than 5 NM from aerodromes listed in the CFS or CWAS
* Operations over unpopulated areas (with a Safety Assurance Declaration) and sparsely populated areas (small RPA with a Pre-Validated Declaration)

**What L1C does NOT authorize:**

* BVLOS in controlled airspace (Class C, D, or E)
* BVLOS over populated areas
* Operations above 400 ft AGL
* Operations within 5 NM of aerodromes
* Carriage of dangerous goods
* Operations with RPA over 150 kg

> **Key Concept — The BVLOS Paradigm Shift:** In VLOS operations, your eyes are your primary safety system. You can see traffic, terrain, weather, and your aircraft's attitude in real time. In BVLOS, *every single one of those inputs disappears or is replaced by an instrument or system.* This course is fundamentally about preparing you to make safe, informed decisions when you can no longer see your aircraft.

### 1.2 The Regulatory Context — Why L1C Exists

Canada's RPAS regulations are structured as a progressive framework that matches operational privileges to demonstrated competence and risk management capability. Understanding where L1C fits in this framework helps you understand *why* the training requirements are what they are.

**The Five Tiers of Canadian RPAS Operations:**

**Tier 1 — Basic Operations (CARs Division IV)**
The entry point. Fly a small drone in uncontrolled airspace, VLOS only, at least 30 m from bystanders. Requires only an online exam based on TP 15263. Minimum age 14. No flight review, no manufacturer declaration. This tier exists for low-risk recreational and simple commercial VLOS work.

**Tier 2 — Advanced Operations (CARs Division V)**
Closer to people, in controlled airspace, and now including EVLOS (with a visual observer within 2 NM) and sheltered BVLOS (within 200 ft horizontal / 100 ft vertical of structures). Requires an Advanced exam, flight review, and an RPAS with a valid Safety Assurance Declaration. Also covers medium RPA VLOS operations. Minimum age 16.

**Tier 3 — Level 1 Complex Operations (CARs Division VI)** ← *You are here*
Routine BVLOS in the lowest-risk BVLOS environment: uncontrolled airspace, low altitude, away from aerodromes, over areas with low population density. Requires an Advanced exam pass, 20 hours of instructor-led ground school (this course), a dedicated L1C exam, a practical flight review, and an RPAS Operator Certificate (RPOC). Minimum age 18.

**Tier 4 — Level 2 Complex Operations (CARs Division VII)**
*Reserved but not yet defined.* Industry expects this will cover higher-risk BVLOS: populated areas, controlled airspace, or operations above 400 ft. Currently requires an SFOC.

**Tier 5 — Level 3 Complex Operations (CARs Divisions VIII–IX)**
*Reserved but not yet defined.* Expected to address highly autonomous, urban, or high-risk operations. Currently requires an SFOC.

**Anything beyond Level 1 Complex** — including BVLOS over populated areas, in controlled airspace, above 400 ft, with aircraft over 150 kg, at advertised events, or by foreign operators — currently requires a Special Flight Operations Certificate under CARs Subpart 3 (Section 903).

> **Discussion Prompt #1:** *"Think about the types of BVLOS missions you want to conduct. Which of them fit within L1C boundaries? Which ones would still require an SFOC? What does this tell you about Transport Canada's approach to risk management?"*

### 1.3 The 3P Framework — Pilot, Procedure, Platform

Transport Canada structures Level 1 Complex operations around three pillars that must ALL be in place before any BVLOS flight:

**PILOT — Level 1 Complex Pilot Certificate (CARs 901.89–901.91)**
The person at the controls must hold a valid L1C pilot certificate, be at least 18 years old, and have met recency requirements within the previous 24 months. A person under 18 or without the certificate may participate in L1C operations only under the *direct supervision* of an L1C certificate holder.

**PROCEDURE — RPAS Operator Certificate / RPOC (CARs 901.88, Division XII)**
The operation must be conducted under the authority of an RPOC. This means the pilot must be the RPAS operator, or an employee / agent / representative of an RPAS operator who holds an RPOC. The RPOC requires an RPAS Operations Manual covering roles, responsibilities, safety processes, normal and emergency procedures, training program, and maintenance procedures.

**PLATFORM — Safety Assurance Declaration (CARs 901.95, Standard 922)**
The RPAS itself must hold a valid manufacturer declaration appropriate for the intended L1C operation. This is not a one-size-fits-all declaration — the type of declaration required depends on the aircraft weight class and the specific operation:

| Operation                                                         | Small RPA (250g–25kg)           | Medium RPA (25–150kg)               |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| BVLOS, ≥1 km from populated area                                  | SAD 922.08(1,2) + 922.09–922.11 | SAD 922.08(3,4,5,6) + 922.09–922.11 |
| BVLOS, \<1 km from populated area OR over sparsely populated area | PVD 922.07 + 922.09–922.12      | Not permitted under L1C             |

*SAD = Safety Assurance Declaration; PVD = Pre-Validated Declaration*

> **Key Concept — All Three Must Align:** You cannot fly BVLOS with a valid pilot certificate on an undeclared aircraft. You cannot fly BVLOS with a declared aircraft but no RPOC. You cannot operate under an RPOC without a qualified pilot. If any one of the three P's is missing, the operation is not legal under Division VI.

## SECTION 2: Your Certification Pathway

### 2.1 The Six Steps to Your L1C Certificate

The path from where you are now to holding a Level 1 Complex pilot certificate follows a specific sequence mandated by Transport Canada:

**Step 1 — Be at least 18 years old**
There is no waiver for this requirement. Unlike Basic (14+) and Advanced (16+), Level 1 Complex operations carry sufficient risk that TC requires full legal adulthood.

**Step 2 — Pass the Advanced Operations exam**
You must have successfully completed the "Remotely Piloted Aircraft Systems — Advanced Operations" examination per TP 15263. You do NOT need to hold an Advanced pilot certificate — you just need to have passed the exam. This ensures L1C candidates have a baseline of VLOS knowledge before building BVLOS competence.

**Step 3 — Complete 20 hours of instructor-led ground school**
This is where you are right now. The ground school must be delivered by a training provider who has self-declared compliance with Standard 921.08, and must cover all subjects in TP 15530. The 20 hours is a *minimum* — this course exceeds that minimum because we believe deeper understanding produces safer pilots.

**Step 4 — Pass the L1C written exam**
After completing ground school, you will write the Transport Canada Level 1 Complex Operations exam through the Drone Management Portal. The exam consists of 50 multiple-choice questions, with a 90-minute time limit and an 80% pass mark (40 out of 50). If you do not pass, you may rewrite after 24 hours.

**Step 5 — Complete the in-person flight review**
Within 12 months before your certificate application, you must complete a flight review with a qualified Flight Reviewer. The flight review is a practical assessment — not a training exercise. The reviewer will evaluate your ability to conduct site surveys, plan missions, perform pre-flight checks, fly by both visual and GCS reference, manage emergencies, and demonstrate CRM if crew members are present.

**Step 6 — Apply for your L1C pilot certificate**
Submit your application through the Drone Management Portal. The certificate costs $125. You will also need an RPOC ($125) before conducting operations.

### 2.2 The Written Exam — What to Expect

The TC exam is administered directly by Transport Canada through the Drone Management Portal — your training provider does not administer or have access to exam questions. This protects exam integrity and ensures all candidates are assessed against the same standard.

**Exam format:**

* 50 multiple-choice questions
* 90-minute time limit
* 80% pass mark (40/50)
* Administered online via the Drone Management Portal
* Available in English or French (no translation assistance permitted)

**Exam content areas (mapped to TP 15530):**

1. Air Law, Air Traffic Rules and Procedures
2. RPA Airframes, Power Plants, Propulsion, and Systems
3. Human Factors
4. Meteorology
5. Navigation
6. Flight Operations
7. Theory of Flight
8. Radiotelephony (ROC-A is also highly recommended)

**Important details:**

* If you fail, you may rewrite after 24 hours
* Your results letter will identify *general subject areas* where you answered incorrectly, but will NOT reveal specific questions
* You are advised to have pencil and paper available during the exam for calculations and diagrams
* Copying, sharing, or receiving help during the exam is prohibited under CARs 901.58, 901.67, and 901.95

> **Instructor Note:** Emphasize that the exam tests *application*, not just memorization. Students should expect questions like "Given these conditions, can this L1C operation proceed?" rather than simple definition recall. Each module in this course includes practice questions at the level of difficulty they should expect.

### 2.3 Recency Requirements

After obtaining your L1C certificate, you must maintain currency by meeting recency requirements within every 24-month period (CARs 901.91). You can satisfy recency through any one of:

* Attending a safety seminar endorsed by Transport Canada
* Completing an RPAS recurrent training program covering human factors, environmental factors, route planning, operations near aerodromes, and applicable regulations
* Completing TC's endorsed self-paced study program (available on the TC drone safety website)
* Writing and passing any RPAS pilot exam (Basic, Advanced, or L1C) — but note that *failing* an exam attempted for recency purposes will NOT meet the requirement

> **Discussion Prompt #2:** *"Recency requirements exist because knowledge degrades over time, regulations change, and complacency develops. Why do you think TC requires ongoing education rather than just logging flight hours? How does this compare to manned aviation recency?"*

### 3.3 The SORA Connection

You will hear us reference **SORA** — the Specific Operations Risk Assessment — throughout this course. SORA is the JARUS risk framework that Transport Canada used to define the boundaries of Level 1 Complex operations. Understanding SORA is NOT required for your L1C exam, but we teach it because:

1. **It explains the "why" behind every L1C restriction.** Why 400 ft? Why uncontrolled airspace only? Why 5 NM from aerodromes? Why unpopulated and sparsely populated areas? Every one of these boundaries was derived from SORA's ground risk and air risk analysis.
2. **It prepares you for Level 2, Level 3, and SFOC operations.** As the regulatory framework expands, SORA will become increasingly central to your work. Any SFOC application for operations beyond L1C already effectively requires a SORA-based Operational Risk Assessment per AC 903-001.
3. **It makes you a better decision-maker.** Pilots who understand the risk model behind their regulations don't just follow rules — they understand *consequences*. When you're 8 km from your GCS in a BVLOS mission and conditions are changing, understanding risk at a fundamental level is what keeps people safe.

## SECTION 4: Key Definitions & Terminology

Before we begin the technical modules, you need a precise understanding of the terminology used throughout this course. These are not just vocabulary words — many of these definitions have specific legal meaning in the CARs and using them incorrectly can create operational and legal problems.

### 4.1 Core RPAS Definitions (CARs 101.01)

**Remotely Piloted Aircraft (RPA):** A navigable aircraft, other than a balloon, rocket, or kite, that is operated by a pilot who is not on board. Note: this is the *aircraft*, not the system.

**Remotely Piloted Aircraft System (RPAS):** A set of configurable elements consisting of a remotely piloted aircraft, its control station, the command and control links, and any other system elements required during flight operation. Note: this is the *entire system*, including everything on the ground.

**Medium Remotely Piloted Aircraft:** An RPA that has an operating weight of more than 25 kg but not more than 150 kg.

**Small Remotely Piloted Aircraft:** An RPA that has an operating weight of more than 250 g but not more than 25 kg. (Defined by exclusion from micro and medium categories.)

**Beyond Visual Line of Sight (BVLOS):** The operation of an RPA at a distance where the pilot or a visual observer can no longer maintain unaided visual contact with the aircraft sufficient to monitor its flight path in relation to other aircraft, persons, vehicles, vessels, and structures for the purpose of avoiding collisions.

**Visual Line of Sight (VLOS):** The operation of an RPA within a distance where the pilot can maintain unaided visual contact (other than corrective lenses) with the aircraft sufficient to monitor its flight path in relation to other aircraft, persons, vehicles, vessels, and structures.

**Extended Visual Line of Sight (EVLOS):** A VLOS operation that uses one or more visual observers, each within 2 NM of the RPA, who maintain visual contact with the aircraft and communicate with the pilot to maintain situational awareness. (This is an Advanced operation, not L1C.)

### 4.2 Population Density Definitions

These definitions are operationally critical for L1C because they determine which operations you can conduct and which declarations your RPAS needs:

**Populated Area:** An area with a population density of more than 25 persons per square kilometre. L1C operations with a SAD must be at least 1 km away from populated areas. L1C operations with a PVD may operate closer to (but not over) populated areas.

**Sparsely Populated Area:** An area with a population density of more than 5 but not more than 25 persons per square kilometre. Small RPA with a PVD may operate over sparsely populated areas under L1C.

**Unpopulated Area:** An area with a population density of 5 or fewer persons per square kilometre. Both SAD and PVD operations are permitted over unpopulated areas.

> **Key Concept — Population Density is Not Intuitive:** A single farmhouse in a large area might be in an "unpopulated" zone statistically, but if your flight path takes you directly over that farmhouse, the *localized* risk is significant. SORA uses maximum population density in the operational footprint, not averages. Your site survey and flight planning must account for the actual distribution of people, not just statistical classification.

### 4.3 Operational Volume Terminology

**Operational Volume:** The three-dimensional space within which the RPA is intended to operate during normal conditions. This is defined by your flight plan.

**Contingency Volume:** An additional buffer surrounding the operational volume that accounts for the possibility that the RPA may deviate from normal operations — for example, during a lost-link recovery manoeuvre or wind gust. This volume must also be assessed for ground and air risk.

**Ground Risk Buffer:** The area on the ground that could be affected if the RPA departs its operational volume. This is determined by the aircraft's kinetic energy, glide performance, and any containment systems (parachutes, geofencing).

## SECTION 5: Understanding the Regulatory Hierarchy

### 5.1 How Canadian Aviation Law is Structured

As an L1C pilot, you operate within a layered legal framework. Understanding this hierarchy matters because when there's a conflict or ambiguity, you need to know which document takes precedence:

**Level 1 — The Aeronautics Act (Federal Statute)**
The foundational law. Grants the Minister of Transport authority to regulate aviation in Canada. Establishes offences, penalties, and enforcement powers. You must have a basic understanding of the Act.

**Level 2 — Canadian Aviation Regulations / CARs (SOR/96-433)**
The detailed regulations made under the authority of the Act. Part IX covers RPAS specifically. These are *law* — non-compliance can result in fines, certificate suspension, or criminal charges. CARs are organized by Part, Division, and Section (e.g., CARs 901.87 is Part IX, Subpart 1, Division VI).

**Level 3 — Standards (e.g., Standard 921, Standard 922, Standard 923)**
Standards are referenced in the CARs and have the force of regulation. Standard 921 covers pilot certification and training requirements. Standard 922 covers RPAS safety assurance declarations. Standard 923 covers vision-based Detect and Avoid. These provide the technical details that the CARs reference.

**Level 4 — Advisory Circulars (e.g., AC 903-001, AC 901-002, AC QUA-001)**
Advisory Circulars describe *acceptable means of compliance* with the regulations. They are not law themselves, but following an AC provides a "safe harbour" — TC will accept that you are in compliance if you follow the applicable AC. You may deviate from an AC if you can demonstrate equivalent safety.

**Level 5 — Transport Publications (e.g., TP 15530, TP 14371/AIM, TP 15263)**
These provide guidance, knowledge requirements, and operational information. TP 15530 defines what your ground school must cover. The AIM (TP 14371) provides operational guidance for RPAS. These inform your practice but are not directly enforceable as law.

> **Discussion Prompt #3:** *"If an Advisory Circular says one thing and a regulation says another, which do you follow? What if your company's RPAS Operations Manual has a stricter procedure than the regulation requires — can you argue the regulation allows you to do less?"*

## SECTION 6: The RPAS Operator Certificate (RPOC)

### 6.1 Why an RPOC Matters

Unlike Basic and Advanced operations, Level 1 Complex operations MUST be conducted under the authority of an RPAS Operator Certificate. This is a deliberate design choice by Transport Canada — BVLOS operations require organizational processes (documented procedures, training programs, maintenance programs, safety management) that a solo pilot certificate doesn't address.

The RPOC is issued to the *operator* (which can be an individual or an organization), not to the pilot. If you are an independent operator, you will be both the RPOC holder and the L1C pilot. If you work for a company, the company holds the RPOC and you operate under its authority.

### 6.2 RPOC Requirements (CARs Division XII)

To hold an RPOC, you need:

**An RPAS Operations Manual (CARs 901.217)** that documents:

* Roles and responsibilities of all crew members
* Roles and responsibilities of all operational and maintenance personnel
* Safety program/processes (or full SMS depending on operation complexity)
* Normal and emergency procedures
* Training program and personnel qualifications
* Maintenance program

**A Maintenance Control Manual (CARs 901.221)** that covers:

* RPA servicing and ground handling procedures
* Maintenance schedules and procedures
* Record-keeping requirements

**A Training Program (CARs 901.219)** that includes:

* Indoctrination training for new personnel
* Initial and annual training for all operational roles
* Competency evaluations to assess training effectiveness
* Documentation within the RPAS Operations Manual

### 6.3 Quality Assurance

RPOC holders must maintain quality assurance processes as described in AC QUA-001. The six QAP elements are: management control, QAP documentation, qualified auditors, an audit program, a finding and corrective action process, and record keeping. TC conducts risk-based compliance inspections on RPOC holders.

### 10.2 After Ground School

* You will write the TC L1C exam through the Drone Management Portal (not administered by us)
* You will need to complete an in-person flight review within 12 months of your certificate application
* You will need to apply for (or already hold) an RPOC before conducting L1C operations
* Your RPAS must hold the appropriate Safety Assurance Declaration for your intended operations

### 10.3 How to Get the Most From This Course

* **Ask questions.** BVLOS operations have nuances that only surface through discussion.
* **Think operationally.** For every regulation or concept, ask yourself: "How does this affect my actual flights?"
* **Connect the modules.** Air law, systems, human factors, meteorology, navigation, operations, theory of flight, and radio are taught separately but they all happen simultaneously on every BVLOS mission.
* **Practice with the knowledge checks.** The TC exam tests application, not just recall. If you can answer the analysis-level questions in each module, the exam will be straightforward.
